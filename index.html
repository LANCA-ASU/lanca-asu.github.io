Research

Our broad aim is to leverage advances in theoretical aerodynamics, computing, and scientific machine learning to better understand complex, nonlinear fluid phenomena, and to develop engineering and design methodologies for the same. We work across three connected threads:

Theory

Unsteady thin-airfoil theory, lifting-line methods, and matched asymptotic expansions for problems where conventional steady aerodynamics breaks down.

Methods

Discrete-vortex models augmented with the leading-edge suction parameter (LESP), open-source low-order solvers, and viscous–inviscid coupling for unsteady flows.

Applications

Flapping and perching micro air vehicles, vertical-axis wind turbines, flapping-foil tidal energy harvesters, and aeroelastic flutter analysis.
