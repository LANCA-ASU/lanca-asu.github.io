Research

We work at the intersection of theoretical aerodynamics, vortex dynamics, and scientific machine learning to develop fast, predictive models for unsteady flows that conventional methods cannot capture.

Approach

Our research method is an integrated approach that uses aerodynamic theory, applied mathematics, computations, and experiments. We derive new mathematical theory and low- to medium-fidelity numerical methods for unsteady flows, while collaborating closely with research groups performing CFD and experiments on the same problems.

In regimes where pure theory cannot capture nonlinear effects such as flow separation, we augment it with numerical calculations based on vortex methods to account for the physics that theory neglects. We call this phenomenological augmentation of theoretical methods.

Themes

Unsteady airfoil theory

Extensions of classical thin-airfoil theory to large amplitudes and frequencies, matched asymptotic expansions for the leading-edge flow, and corrected wake vorticity shedding in time-stepping formulations.

Vortex methods and the LESP framework

Discrete-vortex models that use the leading-edge suction parameter (LESP) to predict the initiation, intensity, and termination of leading-edge vortex shedding. These methods deliver fast, predictive simulations of dynamic stall and flapping-wing aerodynamics.

Three-dimensional unsteady flows

Unsteady lifting-line theory for finite wings, with extensions to swept and morphing geometries. Applications include flapping flight, perching, and wingtip vortex modelling.

Scientific machine learning

Universal Differential Equations (UDEs) and physics-informed approaches that augment our theoretical models with learnable structure, extending closed-form solutions to general engineering problems.

Aeroelasticity and energy harvesting

Self-sustained limit-cycle oscillations, vortex-induced vibration of slender structures, and flapping-foil energy harvesters for tidal and wind applications.
